## Why Heat Heals

### The Science of Contrast

You have probably heard about cold plunges by now. Maybe you have scrolled past someone on social media gasping in an ice bath, promising it will change your life.

Here is what is actually happening in your body when you alternate between hot and cold:

**In the heat** (15-20 minutes, 80-100°C):
- Heart rate rises to match moderate exercise
- Blood vessels dilate, increasing circulation
- Heat shock proteins activate, protecting cells from stress
- Cortisol drops. The nervous system settles.

**In the cold** (30 seconds to 3 minutes, below 15°C):
- Norepinephrine surges—up to 530% in some studies
- Dopamine increases by 250%, lasting for hours
- Blood vessels constrict, then rebound when you warm up
- Inflammation markers decrease

**The magic is in the cycle.** Neither heat nor cold alone produces what the combination does. The repeated dilation and constriction trains your vascular system. The hormonal cascade rewires your baseline.

But here is the part the biohackers miss: the benefit is not just chemical.

It is the practice of choosing discomfort. The threshold moment when you decide to step into the cold anyway. That is where something deeper happens.

## Where to Find It

### Ribersborgs Kallbadhus, Malmö

Some places understand contrast therapy in their bones.

Ribersborgs Kallbadhus has stood on the Swedish coast since 1898. An open-air bathhouse where the practice is simple: heat in the wood-fired sauna, then walk down the pier and into the Öresund strait.

No plunge pools. No temperature controls. Just the sea.

In winter, the water hovers around 4°C. In summer, perhaps 18°C. The seasons dictate the practice. You adapt.

The bathhouse is divided into men's and women's sections, both nude. There is a café where you can sit wrapped in a towel, looking out at the water you just emerged from, wondering how something so simple can feel so profound.

Entry costs around 80 SEK (about $8). No reservations needed. Just show up.

*Not near Malmö?* Look for bathhouses, Korean spas, or Russian banyas in your city. Many now offer contrast circuits. Or start at home: sauna session followed by the coldest shower you can stand. The principle is the same.

## Five Minutes of Stillness

### The Threshold

There is a reason contrast therapy works on more than just your body.

Every cycle, you face a small decision. The sauna is warm. Comfortable. Staying would be easy. The cold is... not. Your mind will offer a hundred reasons to skip it. Just this once.

And then you go anyway.

This is a practice in miniature. The willingness to leave comfort. The discovery that discomfort passes faster than you expect. The quiet pride of having done what you said you would do.

Over time, something accumulates. Not just physical resilience, but a kind of trust in yourself.

The Finns do not call this "cold exposure therapy." They just call it Tuesday. The cycle between heat and cold is not an intervention. It is simply how bathing works.

Maybe that is the reframe we need. Not another protocol to optimize. Just a practice. Something you do because it feels right. Because your body was built for this kind of temperature variability. Because 200,000 years of evolution did not prepare you for 72°F all day, every day.

Close your eyes. Feel your breath slow.

The cold is waiting. And so is everything it teaches.

### This Week's Practice

**Try one contrast cycle this week.**

- Heat for 15-20 minutes (sauna, steam room, or hot bath)
- Cold for 30-90 seconds (cold shower, plunge, or outdoor air)
- Rest for 5-10 minutes before repeating

Do not overthink it. Do not time it perfectly. Just notice what happens when you alternate between the two.

That is the practice.
